|STRATEGY_

How to actually do well.

The SAT rewards pattern recognition, not raw intelligence. Tap any topic to expand.

71 sec per RW question · 95 sec per Math question. Know your budget before you sit down.
If stuck after 90 seconds - guess, flag, move on. A skipped question and a wrong answer cost the same. Skipping saves time for questions you can win.
Easy and hard questions are worth identical points. Never spend 3 minutes on one hard question when you could answer 2 easy ones.
Read the question before the passage. Knowing what to look for makes reading faster and more targeted.
Eliminate, don't just pick. The SAT uses "almost right" traps. Cross out clearly wrong choices first.
Vocabulary: Go with the word that fits the passage's specific tone - not the most impressive-sounding one.
Grammar: Read the sentence aloud mentally. The SAT tests a small repeating set of rules - learn them cold.
Show your work. Most errors come from skipping steps on problems you actually know how to solve.
Plug in numbers. When a problem uses variables, substitute real numbers and test which answer works.
Work backwards from answer choices. For "what is x" questions, try plugging in the choices - start with B or C.
Don't skip word problems. Most SAT word problems test simple concepts in context. Set up the equation and the math is usually straightforward.
Pattern recognition, not intelligence. Every question type repeats. The more patterns you know, the faster and more accurate you get.
Review wrong answers more than right ones. Understanding one mistake is worth 10x reviewing questions you already know.
Consistency beats cramming. 30 minutes daily for 8 weeks outperforms any last-minute marathon.

|HIGH_SCHOOL_YEARS_

Four years. Every one counts.

Each year has a name. Colleges and teachers use these terms constantly - know them.

9
Freshman Year
Your first year. The most important thing is establishing good habits and finding your footing. Don't fall behind early - it's much harder to recover later.
10
Sophomore Year
You take the PSAT this year. Start thinking about which AP classes you want in 11th and 12th grade - and which subjects genuinely interest you.
11
Junior Year - The Most Important
Colleges look at this year most closely. You take the SAT or ACT, AP exams, and begin building your college list. Junior year GPA carries the most weight in applications.
12
Senior Year
College applications, decisions, and graduation. Avoid "senioritis" - colleges can and do rescind acceptances if grades drop significantly in the final semester.
|CLASS_LEVELS_

Not all classes are equal.

The level you choose signals to colleges how hard you're pushing yourself. Colleges expect challenge in your strongest subjects.

REGULAR_STANDARD
Regular
GPA weight: 1.0x
The default level. An A = 4.0 unweighted GPA. Fine for most subjects, but colleges expect you to take harder classes where you're capable.
HONORS
Honors
GPA weight: ~1.1x
A step above regular. Faster pace, harder tests, more depth. An A in Honors = 4.5 on a weighted scale at most schools.
AP_ADVANCED_PLACEMENT
AP
GPA weight: ~1.2x · College credit possible
College-level courses in high school. A score of 3-5 on the May AP exam can earn actual college credit - skipping that class in college and saving tuition money. Highly valued by admissions.
IB_INTERNATIONAL_BACCALAUREATE
IB
Internationally recognized
A rigorous international program. Not all schools offer it. If your previous school was IB, those credits may transfer. Extremely well-respected by colleges worldwide.
|SCHOOL_YEAR_

How the year is structured.

The school year runs late August or early September through late May or June - roughly 10 months. Most high schools use two semesters of ~18 weeks each.

Semester 1
September to January. Ends with final exams before winter break.
Semester 2
January to May/June. Ends with final exams and AP exams in May.
Summer
June through August. No required school, but many students take summer courses, do internships, or build their extracurricular profile.
Breaks
Thanksgiving (late November), Winter Break (~2 weeks Dec/Jan), Spring Break (~1 week March/April).
|CLASSROOM_CULTURE_

What's different here.

If you studied in another country, some things about American classrooms will surprise you.

Participation is graded
In many classes, speaking up in discussion counts toward your grade. Staying silent and acing every test may still leave you with a B instead of an A.
Teachers are approachable
Going to talk to your teacher before or after class is normal and expected. Asking for help is seen as a sign of effort, not weakness.
Academic honesty is taken very seriously
Copying homework, plagiarism, or cheating can result in a zero, suspension, or expulsion. Always cite your sources. Always use your own words.
Extracurriculars matter
Sports, clubs, student government, community service - these are not optional in the eyes of colleges. A 4.0 with no activities is less competitive than a 3.8 with genuine involvement.
Deadlines are firm
Late work is typically penalized or not accepted. Managing your schedule and submitting on time is your responsibility - teachers will not chase you.
|TESTING_TIMELINE_

When to take what.

American students take several major standardized tests throughout high school.

G10
PSAT 10
A practice version of the SAT. Good for getting used to the format. Scores don't count for college admissions.
G11
PSAT/NMSQT + SAT + AP Exams
The PSAT this year counts for National Merit Scholarship eligibility. Take the SAT for the first time in spring. Sit AP exams in your AP subjects in May.
G12
SAT Retakes + AP Exams
SAT retakes if needed in fall. Submit scores with college applications. More AP exams in May.
Any
ACT
An alternative to the SAT. Same purpose, slightly different format - includes a Science section. Most colleges accept both equally.
|NEW_TO_THE_US_

Specifically for students new here.

Coming from another country adds an extra layer of complexity. These are the most common things new students don't know.

ESL / ELL Programs
If English isn't your first language, most schools offer ESL/ELL support. Don't be embarrassed to use it - it's designed to help you succeed faster.
Your credits may transfer
If you took rigorous courses in your home country, the school counselor can evaluate your transcripts and may give you credit for subjects you already completed.
Find your counselor on Day 1
Every student is assigned a school counselor. They help with scheduling, college planning, and navigating the system. Use them early and often - they are one of your most valuable resources.
When you enroll matters
If you arrive mid-year, you may miss the registration window for AP or advanced classes. Ask specifically about this. Some schools allow exceptions for new students.
Public school is free
Public K-12 education in the US is fully funded by taxes - no tuition. Some schools may ask for optional activity fees, but education itself costs nothing.

What Matters Most

Admissions starts with the transcript.

Across four-year colleges, grades in college-prep courses, total grades, and strength of curriculum are consistently the top admission factors. Essays, recommendations, activities, character, and demonstrated interest can decide the outcome once your academics are in range.

1. Academic performanceProtect GPA first. A strong transcript in core classes is more persuasive than one last-minute test score.
2. Rigor with judgmentTake the hardest courses you can handle well. Colleges like challenge, but not a schedule that breaks your grades.
3. Proof of directionActivities, essays, projects, jobs, and recommendations should show what you care about and how you act on it.
Dream University Strategy

Build a profile that makes sense.

Match your spike to the school.If a school is known for engineering, business, medicine, public policy, or art, your application should show evidence that you are already moving in that direction.
Use the reach-target-safety system.A healthy list usually has 3-4 reach schools, 4-6 target schools, and 2-3 likely schools you would genuinely attend.
Do not chase prestige blindly.Fit includes cost, location, teaching style, class size, majors, support, outcomes, and whether you can picture yourself doing real work there.
Make every essay specific.Replace generic praise with concrete details: courses, labs, professors, student groups, campus programs, and why those connect to your goals.

Reach

Your stats are below or near the middle range, or the admit rate is very low. Apply only when your story is unusually strong.

Aim for 3-4.

Target

Your grades, rigor, and scores are in range. Still not guaranteed, but your application has a realistic path.

Aim for 4-6.

Likely

You are above the typical admitted range and can afford it. A likely school should still be a place you would be happy to attend.

Aim for 2-3 you would attend.

What Is GPA?

GPA stands for Grade Point Average - a single number representing your academic performance across all classes. Most US schools use a 4.0 scale, where A = 4.0 and F = 0.

Colleges treat GPA as one of their primary filters. A strong GPA shows consistency - not just that you can do well once, but that you perform reliably across every subject over multiple years.

Weighted vs Unweighted GPA

There are two types of GPA - and both matter.

Unweighted
Max = 4.0. Every class is equal - Regular, Honors, and AP all treated the same.
A in Regular = 4.0
A in AP = 4.0
Weighted
Max = 4.5-5.0. Harder classes earn bonus points. Rewards taking challenges.
A in Regular = 4.0
A in AP = 5.0
Which matters more?Most colleges recalculate GPA with their own formula. What matters: are you taking hard classes AND doing well in them? A 3.7 unweighted in all AP is more impressive than a 4.0 in all standard classes.

What Colleges Actually Look At
Course rigor over raw GPA A 3.8 with all AP/Honors is typically better than a 4.0 in all standard classes. Colleges want to see you challenge yourself.
Upward trend matters Grades improving from 9th to 11th grade is a positive signal. A downward trend - even with a good overall GPA - raises concerns.
Junior year is weighted most When you apply in fall of senior year, junior grades are the most recent full year colleges have. They carry the most weight.
Relevant subjects for your major Engineering ->strong Math and Physics. Pre-med ->Biology and Chemistry. Business ->Economics and Statistics. Excel in what matters for your goals.
How to Choose Your Courses
Take the hardest classes you can get a B or better in
An A in standard is worth less than a B in AP. But a C in AP is not better than an A in Honors. Know your limits.
Align classes with your intended major
Pre-med ->AP Bio and AP Chem. Engineering ->AP Physics and AP Calc. Business ->AP Econ and AP Stats. Match your schedule to your goals.
Quality over quantity
7 AP classes with B's and C's is worse than 4 with A's. Burnout hurts your GPA, mental health, and extracurriculars all at once.
Don't take only easy classes to protect GPA
A 4.0 with no AP or Honors sends a red flag to selective colleges. They see your full course list - ease is noticed.

Class Rank - Does It Matter?

Class rankis your position relative to other students in your graduating class by GPA. Ranked 15th out of 400 = top 4%.

Not all schools report it.Many have stopped because it can be discouraging. If your school doesn't rank, colleges don't penalize you.
Top 10% matters for some programs.UT Austin's Texas Top 10% rule admits students based purely on rank. Know if this applies to you.
Weighted rank is common.Taking AP classes can boost your rank even if your unweighted GPA isn't perfect.
